# PRODUCT_PLAYBOOK.md — randomorium

This is the creative bible for randomorium. Read this before building anything user-facing — an app, a feature, a line of copy. It defines what makes something "randomorium" and what doesn't.

---

## What Makes a Randomorium App

A randomorium app is:

- **Small.** One page, one idea, one session. If it needs onboarding, it's too big.
- **Immediately obvious.** A visitor should understand what it does within 5 seconds of landing.
- **Funny, surprising, or delightful.** It provokes a reaction. Not necessarily a belly laugh — a raised eyebrow counts.
- **A hat excuse.** There's a natural, amusing connection between the app and buying an embroidered cap.
- **Built by one person.** Each app has a single author. It reflects their sense of humour, not a committee's.

A randomorium app is NOT:

- A SaaS product with accounts, dashboards, and pricing tiers
- A tool people need to use regularly (if they come back, great — but don't design for retention)
- A tech demo showing off a framework or library (the tech is invisible; the experience is everything)
- A clone of something that already exists (weird originals only)
- A group project (one person, one app, one vibe)

## The Delight Budget

Every app gets a **delight budget** — and it must be spent, not saved.

At minimum, each app needs **one moment of genuine delight**. This is the moment that makes someone screenshot it, text the link, or say "wait, what?" out loud. It's not optional. It's the entire reason the app exists.

### What counts as a delight moment

- An unexpected result that makes the user laugh
- A visual surprise (animation, reveal, transformation)
- Copy that's so self-aware it breaks the fourth wall
- An interaction that feels different from every other website
- A punchline the user didn't see coming

### What doesn't count

- Smooth animations (those are polish, not delight)
- Good design (that's baseline, not a moment)
- Working correctly (that's a requirement, not a feature)

### Examples of delight moments

- A holiday planner where the salesman is a snake charmer who hisses through the pitch
- A random generator that occasionally generates something impossibly specific
- An app that confidently gives you terrible advice with complete sincerity
- A quiz where the results are all hats

The delight moment is the thing people remember. Build the app around it.

## The Hat Hook Doctrine

Every app must connect to the hat shop. The hat hook is how.

### Three criteria for a good hat hook

1. **Natural.** It feels like part of the app's world, not an ad bolted on.
2. **Amusing.** The connection between the app and hats is funny or surprising.
3. **Clickable.** It makes someone curious enough to visit the shop.

### Patterns that work

| Pattern | Example |
|---------|---------|
| Character recommendation | "Sssalem recommends a holiday hat before you travel" |
| Contextual result | The app's output includes a hat suggestion as part of its logic |
| Self-aware confession | "This app exists to sell hats. The rest is a beautiful lie." |
| Earned reward | Complete the app's interaction and get a hat recommendation as a prize |
| Thematic alignment | App about randomness → "Random hat for a random person" |

### Patterns that don't work

| Pattern | Why it fails |
|---------|-------------|
| Generic CTA | "Buy a hat" — no personality, no connection to the app |
| Interruption | Pop-up mid-experience — breaks immersion, feels desperate |
| Forced placement | Hat reference that contradicts the app's internal logic |
| Over-explanation | Two paragraphs about why you should buy a hat — the joke dies |

## The Core Emotion

The core emotion of randomorium is:

> **"This is delightfully absurd."**

Not hilarious — delightfully absurd. Not weird for weird's sake — delightful. The sweet spot is when something is strange enough to be memorable but warm enough to be shareable.

### The emotional spectrum

| Too cold | Just right | Too hot |
|----------|-----------|---------|
| "That's weird" | "That's delightfully absurd" | "That's trying too hard" |
| Confusing | Surprising | Exhausting |
| Alienating | Inclusive | Exclusive |
| Random | Intentionally random | Chaotic |

### Secondary emotions

- **Shareability.** "I need to send this to someone." The app triggers a social reflex.
- **The hat reveal.** "Oh wait, this is a hat shop?" A moment of amused recognition when the visitor discovers the real purpose.
- **Gentle conversion.** "Honestly, I might buy that hat." The funnel works because it's charming, not pushy.

## Design Principles

### 1. Surprise > Efficiency
A randomorium app should make someone pause and react. Efficiency is for productivity tools. We build experiences, not workflows.

### 2. Personality > Polish
Ship with character before shipping with shine. A rough app with a great voice beats a polished app with no soul. Polish comes second. Personality comes first.

### 3. Memorable > Functional
Nobody remembers a functional app. People remember the one that made them laugh, confused them in a good way, or sold them a hat through a snake charmer. Optimise for the story someone tells about it later.

### 4. Simple > Complete
One feature, done well, with one great moment. Don't add a settings page, a dark mode toggle, or user accounts. The app does one thing. If it needs more, it's a different app.

### 5. Self-aware > Sincere
randomorium knows it exists to sell hats. The apps know they're absurd. The copy knows it's copy. This self-awareness is the brand's secret weapon — it makes the sales pitch feel like a joke you're in on, not a trick being played on you.

### 6. Inclusive > Inside
The humour should work for everyone, not just people who get the reference. No in-jokes that require context. No memes that expire. The absurdity should be universally accessible. A 12-year-old and a 60-year-old should both understand why it's funny.

## Design Checklist

### Before writing any code

- [ ] **One sentence.** Can you describe the app in one sentence that makes someone curious?
- [ ] **Delight moment.** What's the one moment that makes someone react? Write it down.
- [ ] **Hat hook.** How does this app connect to the hat shop? Write the hat hook sentence.
- [ ] **Would you text it?** If a friend texted you this link with no context, would you click it and enjoy it?

### After building

- [ ] **Test on phone.** Open it on a real phone. Does it work? Is the text readable? Can you tap everything?
- [ ] **Read copy aloud.** Every piece of text. Out loud. Does it sound like randomorium? Is it too long?
- [ ] **Click the hat link.** Does HatBanner render? Does the shop link work? Is the hat hook on the app card?
- [ ] **Show it to someone uninvolved.** Not a teammate. Someone with no context. Watch their face. Did they react?

## Voice & Tone Guide

### The voice

randomorium's voice is **dry, self-aware, warm, and confident**.

- **Dry, not loud.** The humour is understated. We don't use exclamation marks to signal that something is funny. If it's funny, the reader will know.
- **Self-aware, not ironic.** We know we're selling hats through absurd apps. We say it plainly. The self-awareness is part of the charm.
- **Warm, not cold.** Absurdist but never mean. The humour includes the reader, never targets them. Everyone's in on the joke.
- **Confident, not desperate.** We don't beg for clicks, follows, or purchases. We make something weird, put it online, and let it speak for itself.

### On-brand copy

| Context | On-brand | Off-brand |
|---------|----------|-----------|
| Hero tagline | "random apps. ironic hats." | "Welcome to randomorium! We make fun apps!" |
| App description | "Plan a group holiday with a snake charmer salesman" | "Holiday Bazaar is a fun and interactive holiday planning tool" |
| Shop CTA | "Buy an ironic hat." | "Shop our exclusive collection!" |
| Error page | "Something went wrong. Probably our fault. Definitely not the hats' fault." | "Oops! An error occurred. Please try again." |
| Empty state | "apps loading... check back soon." | "No apps available at this time." |
| Hat hook | "Sssalem recommends a holiday hat before you travel" | "Don't forget to check out our hats!" |
| Footer | "harry · matt · sol" | "© 2025 Randomorium Inc. All rights reserved." |

### Words we use
random, weird, absurd, ironic, embroidered, cap, hat, probably, honestly, apparently, somehow

### Words we don't use
amazing, awesome, incredible, revolutionary, innovative, leverage, synergy, ecosystem, empower, unlock

### Punctuation
- Lowercase sentence case by default (not Title Case)
- Periods at the end of statements, not exclamation marks
- Em dashes for asides — like this
- Ellipses sparingly... and only for comic timing
- The → arrow in CTAs ("buy a hat →")
